# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Experienced developers who supervise several coding agents, shells, services,
and logs at once. They work primarily in a terminal, often across worktrees or
SSH, and need to understand what is running, what is blocked, and what is proven
without hunting through tabs.

## Product Purpose

Nagi is terminal-native mission control for parallel coding agents. It keeps
work alive, isolates each mission, centralizes the moments that need a human,
and requires fresh, inspectable evidence before work can be called complete.
Success means one calm surface can take a user from an objective to a reviewed
result without replacing their agent, shell, or terminal.

## Brand Personality

Calm, tactile, assured. Nagi should feel light and human, with quiet Japanese
precision. It should inspire the confidence of a serious native tool without
looking like generic developer software or cyberpunk terminal cosplay.

## Anti-references

- Dense monitoring dashboards that make parallel work feel more stressful.
- Fake terminal chrome, neon grids, glow effects, and decorative hacker motifs.
- Web-dashboard patterns forced into a terminal, especially stacked cards and
  permanent navigation that steals working space.
- Ornamental Japanese stereotypes or unexplained kanji.
- Interfaces that communicate status through color alone.
- Plugin systems that imply an authority boundary the runtime cannot enforce.

## Design Principles

- Make the next human decision obvious before exposing secondary detail.
- Show operational truth, not activity theatre or decorative metrics.
- Preserve terminal speed and density while progressively revealing complexity.
- Use one coherent interaction vocabulary across keyboard, mouse, and SSH.
- Let customization change expression without weakening semantics or safety.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast, complete keyboard operation, redundant state cues,
reduced-motion behavior, and layouts that remain legible from a phone-sized SSH
client through a wide desktop terminal. Mouse support must complement, never
replace, the keyboard path.
